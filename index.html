Featured Project

Air New Zealand
Premium Proposition Review

January to March 2026
Mixed Methods Analysis
Routes: AKL-SYD and AKL-SIN
17-Slide Report
Research Question
Is Air NZ's premium passenger experience keeping pace with its premium pricing — and what is the loyalty risk if it isn't?
This analysis does not conclude that Air NZ is uniquely underperforming. It identifies specific route-level pricing and experience dynamics that warrant attention in a globally competitive premium environment — particularly as competitors reinvest and fleet constraints temporarily compress capacity and reliability metrics. Yield normalisation, uneven corporate travel recovery, and industry-wide engine constraints are documented trends for the analysis period and are accounted for in the analytical framework.

Five Key Findings

Finding 01
Pricing — Emerging Route Divergence
Air NZ is priced 5.89% below Qantas on AKL-SYD and cannot command a price premium on its most competitive route. Materiality threshold of 5% met. Source: point-in-time fare collection, consistent with three-year yield trend from Air NZ Annual Reports FY2023 to FY2025.
Finding 02
Observed Product-Price Mismatch — AKL-SIN
Air NZ is priced 9.53% above Singapore Airlines on AKL-SIN. Observed product indicators — fare flexibility, aircraft type, satisfaction rating — favour SQ at the lower price point. Materiality threshold met. Source: fare benchmarking and Skytrax ratings.
Finding 03
Operational Performance — Declining Trend
OTP declined 2.1pp from the 2023 peak to 77.58% in 2024. Advantage over Qantas narrowed from 9.3pp to 3.65pp. The 787 grounding removed an estimated 14,390 Business Premier seats over 209 days, coinciding with the period of explicit yield softening noted in the Air NZ FY2024 Annual Report. Source: Cirium OTP Review 2022 to 2024; Air NZ Press Release March 2024.
Finding 04
Loyalty Economics — Competitive, Increasing Dependency
Airpoints returns 4.89% per flight versus 3.94% for KrisFlyer — programme value remains competitive. Satisfaction rated 5/10 versus SQ 7/10. Loyalty economics may be offsetting variability in experience metrics, creating an increasing retention dependency that warrants ongoing monitoring. Source: Air NZ Annual Reports; Point Hacks; Skytrax.
Finding 05
$82.6M Revenue Exposure at Approximately 3% Churn
Scenario modelling shows commercial exposure of approximately $82.6M NZD annually at 3% premium member churn. The compounding pressure scenario reaches $137.7M at 5% churn. Sensitivity range across reasonable assumption variation is $27.5M to $137.7M. Model assumptions: 250,000 estimated elite members (5% proxy of 5M FY2025 Airpoints members), average fare $2,754, four annual flights per premium passenger. Revenue at risk represents direct fare revenue only. Premium member base is estimated — Air NZ does not publicly disclose elite tier membership separately. All assumptions documented in the full report.

Integrated Finding

Three analytical variables — one commercial conclusion
If premium passengers experience a sustained 2.1pp OTP decline while paying a 9.53% fare premium over Singapore Airlines on AKL-SIN — and passenger satisfaction is already rated 2 points below SQ — revenue vulnerability emerges at only 3% churn, representing $82.6M NZD at risk annually.
Loyalty is retaining passengers despite the experience, not because of it. That is an increasing commercial dependency that warrants monitoring. The degree to which programme value alone sustains high-yield retention is the central risk variable in this analysis.

Analytical Framework

Engine 1
Pricing Analysis
Point-in-time fare benchmarking on AKL-SYD and AKL-SIN, plus three-year yield and load factor trend from Air NZ Annual Reports FY2023 to FY2025. Materiality threshold: price gap greater than 5%.
Engine 2
Reliability Analysis
OTP trend comparison 2022 to 2024 using Cirium data (15-minute threshold standard). 787 grounding capacity impact modelled from Air NZ press release March 2024 and SeatGuru V2 configuration. Materiality: OTP gap greater than 3pp.
Engine 3
Loyalty Sensitivity
Points value and earn rate comparison across Airpoints, Qantas FF and KrisFlyer. Revenue-at-risk churn model across three scenarios: Recovery (1% churn), Plateau (3% churn), and Compounding Pressure (5% churn). All assumptions documented.

Evidence Hierarchy

Tier 1 — Primary
Annual Reports and Official Press Releases
Air NZ Annual Reports FY2023 to FY2025. Air NZ Press Release March 2024 (787 grounding). Air NZ Lounge Investment Announcement 2025. Carries the analytical argument — audited and publicly attributable.
Tier 2 — Supporting
Cirium, Skytrax, Australian Aviation
Cirium OTP data, Skytrax airline and lounge ratings, SeatGuru aircraft configuration. Supports and contextualises Tier 1 findings — not relied upon to carry the argument independently.
Tier 3 — Directional
Consumer Reviews and Points Blogs
Google Maps reviews, Point Hacks valuations, Airlinequality.com. Directional evidence only. Labelled as such throughout the report and not relied upon to carry the analytical argument.
Key Limitations: Fare data is a point-in-time snapshot collected 25 February 2026 and is not an average market price — however, the direction of findings is consistent with the three-year yield trend from audited annual reports. The 787 grounding capacity impact is a modelled estimate with a fully documented methodology. The premium member base is an industry-standard proxy, as Air NZ does not publicly disclose elite tier membership separately. Areas identified for further deep-dive include corporate contract pricing analysis, route-level premium yield segmentation, NPS versus churn correlation, and a more dynamic 787 fleet recovery model. These represent data access constraints rather than methodological gaps — the findings are defensible within documented assumptions.
